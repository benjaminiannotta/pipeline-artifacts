## Domain 1 — Regrets of the dying (Bronnie Ware)

### The claim
A palliative carer recorded five regrets voiced repeatedly by the dying: not living true to oneself, working too hard, not expressing feelings, losing touch with friends, not letting oneself be happier. Summarised as "it all comes down to love and relationships in the end."

### Supporting evidence [PRIMARY]
- bronnieware.com — Ware's own first-hand account, the five regrets, the spine line.
- mindful.org, soundstrue.com, goodlifeproject.com — long-form Ware interviews, consistent retelling.

### Disconfirming pass — THIS CHANGES THINGS
- **It is NOT research, and Ware says so herself.** In her own words: "it's definitely not academic research... I've been called a nurse, and a doctor, and a researcher... that's not the case, and I've been honest about that from the start. It was based on my own experience." [PRIMARY — Sounds True transcript]. She also had no formal qualifications when she entered palliative care.
- **A scientific critique argues the opposite may be true.** A PLOS science blog ("Absolutely Maybe", 2020) argues researchers who cite Ware show confirmation bias, and points to theoretical and empirical evidence that as death nears, people are motivated to feel their regrets are "too few to mention." So the real research on the dying may show FEWER regrets, not a clean top-five. [PRIMARY — PLOS blog by a research-methods writer]
- The book itself is half memoir; even sympathetic readers note a preachy, self-help tone in the second half. The "five regrets" is now a commercial brand (books, workshops), which is a mild incentive-to-believe flag.

### Verdict
**CONTESTED / ANECDOTAL.** The five regrets are a resonant first-hand observation, NOT evidence. They cannot be cited as fact. **Use in the post as one human voice, clearly the voice of someone who sat with the dying, not as proof.** The evidential weight must come from Domains 2 and 4. This is the single most important correction the disconfirming pass produced.

## Domain 2 — What makes a good life (Harvard Study of Adult Development)

### The claim
The longest-running study of human happiness finds that strong, warm relationships — not money, fame, IQ, or class — best predict who is happy and healthy across a life. And people found love and friendship late, when they'd given up expecting to.

### Supporting evidence [PRIMARY]
- news.harvard.edu/gazette, robertwaldinger.com, hsph.harvard.edu, mckinsey Author Talks — director Waldinger, direct quotes, the central relationship finding, the "not too late" late-life finding.
- Began 1938; 724 original men (268 Harvard, 456 inner-city Boston); now 1,300+ descendants across three generations. The relationship finding is the study's most consistent, most stated result across 85 years and multiple directors (Vaillant, then Waldinger).

### Disconfirming pass
- **The original sample is narrow: all male, all white.** The Grant cohort was 268 Caucasian Harvard men; the Glueck cohort 456 Caucasian inner-city men. [PRIMARY — Harvard study metadata, Wikipedia/Grant Study, Health Affairs]. Generalising "what makes *humans* happy" from a sample that excluded all women and all people of colour is a real limitation.
- **Fair counter (also evidenced):** Health Affairs notes "it is tempting to criticise the Grant Study for its highly selective sample of Harvard men, but that was the point," and the team deliberately merged in the disadvantaged Boston cohort to broaden beyond privilege; the study now includes women among the 1,300+ descendants. So the narrowness is real but partly addressed over time.
- The relationship-happiness link is correlational within an observational study; it is strongly and repeatedly observed, but it is not a randomised causal proof.

### Verdict
**PROVEN as the strongest pillar here, WITH a stated limitation.** The relationship finding is robust, long, and replicated within the study across generations. The original-sample narrowness must be acknowledged in this doc (not necessarily on screen). This is the load-bearing evidence the post should rest on — it is what Ware alone cannot carry.

## Domain 3 — Why emotional content gets shared (Berger & Milkman)

### The claim
High-arousal emotion drives sharing; low-arousal sadness suppresses it. So content that moves people and turns toward hope/awe spreads; content that ends in flat sadness does not.

### Supporting evidence [PRIMARY]
- journals.sagepub.com / SSRN — Berger & Milkman, "What Makes Online Content Viral?" (2012): all NYT articles over 3 months (~7,000), human-coded subset (~2,500), logistic regression, PLUS lab experiments for causality. Positive > negative; awe/anger/anxiety (high-arousal) more viral; **sadness (low-arousal) less viral**. Holds controlling for surprise, usefulness, and placement.
- jonahberger.com/Arousal2.pdf — the arousal experiments (93-participant emotion study; 40-participant jogging study, +42% sharing).
- **Replication [PRIMARY]:** ScienceDirect — German study re-ran BM on 27,375 Spiegel articles, "results generally consistent with BM's findings across all channels." Cross-cultural, cross-platform support.

### Disconfirming pass
- **One study explicitly rejects the arousal mechanism.** Yu et al. (arXiv 2011.09003), using the EASI framework, "do not support the theory of arousal increasing social transmission." [PRIMARY — peer paper]. So the mechanism is not unanimous.
- **The "negative high-arousal also spreads" part is shakier than the "positive spreads" part.** A YouTube-video study (Guadagno et al.) found positive emotions increased sharing while negative emotions *reduced* it; funny/cute shared most. So anger/anxiety driving sharing is less settled than the core positive/awe finding.
- **A useful refinement [PRIMARY]:** the German replication found an *inverted-U* between positivity and virality — emotion helps up to a point, then too much hurts. This argues for restraint.

### Verdict
**STRONG but NOT settled.** The specific claim the post relies on — *sadness alone suppresses sharing; turning toward hope/positive emotion is what travels* — is well-supported and replicated. The broader arousal theory has at least one credible dissent, so state it as strong-but-contested, not law. The inverted-U finding independently backs the brief's mandate of restraint ("deliver the heavy truth lightly").

## Domain 4 — The cost of disconnection (Holt-Lunstad)

### The claim
Social connection protects health and longevity; isolation and loneliness raise mortality risk.

### Supporting evidence [PRIMARY]
- journals.plos.org/plosmedicine (2010, 148 studies): being socially connected increases odds of survival by ~50%.
- hsph.harvard.edu (Holt-Lunstad, 2015 review, 3.4M participants): loneliness +26% premature-death risk, social isolation +29%, living alone +32%.

### Disconfirming pass
- **The famous "loneliness = 15 cigarettes a day" line is contested by its own authors.** Holt-Lunstad, Smith & Kawachi (American Journal of Epidemiology, 2023) caution the smoking comparison oversimplifies; other commentary notes smoking 15–20/day may carry a *larger* mortality risk than social isolation, and that the original figure's derivation was unclear. [PRIMARY — AJE commentary + the researcher's own "15 Cigarettes" page]
- The mortality findings are observational/correlational; confounding (e.g. illness causing both isolation and death) is a known challenge, though the meta-analyses attempt to control for it.

### Verdict
**Direction PROVEN, headline figure CONTESTED.** That disconnection harms health is robust. The specific cigarette equivalence must NOT be asserted as fact. Use the human truth (connection protects us, isolation costs us); do not quote "15 cigarettes."

## What is NOT established (the gaps, named)
- That the dying actually hold these five specific regrets in any measured, representative way. (Ware is anecdotal; some evidence suggests fewer regrets near death.) **Do not present the regrets as fact.**
- That the relationship→happiness finding generalises perfectly across gender and race. (Original Harvard sample was white men; broadened only later.)
- That high-arousal *negative* emotion reliably drives sharing. (Mixed; one study rejects the arousal mechanism entirely.)
- That isolation equals "15 cigarettes a day." (Contested by the original authors.)

## Bottom line for the build
The post is honest if it: leans its factual weight on the Harvard finding (D2) and the direction of Holt-Lunstad (D4); uses Ware (D1) as a human voice openly rooted in sitting with the dying, not as data; ends on hope and a small action, which Berger (D3) supports as the shareable shape; and quotes no contested figure (no "15 cigarettes", no invented percentages, no fabricated deathbed quotes). Emotion carried by craft, truth carried by the two solid pillars.
