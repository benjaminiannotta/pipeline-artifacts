**Description:** A cinematic, slow-fade, fully client-side Instagram-style post that moves a stranger through the real human truth that at the end of life it is connection and love that matter, not money or work, then turns to hope and a small action, built to be saved and sent to one person they love.

## 0. Honesty constraint (governs everything below)
This brief was rewritten AFTER its research was completed to the protocol bar. The research changed it. Two corrections are load-bearing and must be honoured by the build:

1. **The "regrets of the dying" are anecdotal, not evidence.** Bronnie Ware states plainly, in her own words, that it is not research, just her experience from sitting with dying people, and there is a scientific critique suggesting people may actually have *fewer* regrets as death nears. So the post must use Ware as **a human voice** (someone who sat at deathbeds), never as proof or fact. Do not phrase the regrets as "studies show" or "people regret X." Phrase them as what one carer heard.
2. **The factual weight rests on the Harvard Study of Adult Development**, the longest study of human happiness, whose clearest finding is that warm relationships, not money or fame, best predict a happy, healthy life. That is the proven pillar. The post leans here for truth.
3. **No contested or invented numbers.** Do not assert "loneliness equals 15 cigarettes a day" (disowned by its own authors). No fabricated deathbed quotes. No invented percentages. Emotion is carried by craft; truth by the solid pillar.

## 1. Concept (one line)
A single-screen, fully client-side cinematic post that walks the viewer line by line, with slow filmic fade-in motion, from a quiet universal opening, through the ache of what we neglect (told as what a hospice carer heard, and what an 85-year study found), to the single converging truth that it comes down to the people we love, then turns to hope (it is not too late) and a small instruction to reach out now, so it moves a stranger enough to save it and send it to one person.

## 2. Core function (the one thing the viewer does)
- One screen, no button to start. The piece auto-plays as a slow vertical sequence of short emotional lines, each fading in over a dark cinematic background, paced like a film title sequence: one line lands, holds, the next arrives.
- Built to feel like a motion-picture moment, not an infographic. Each beat is one short sentence, large, centred, with breathing room.
- The emotional arc moves and MUST end on hope and agency, never on grief (this is both the honest reading of the research and, per Berger, the shape that gets shared): quiet universal hook -> the ache (what we let slip: closeness, honest feeling, time with people, being true to ourselves) -> the converging truth (an 85-year study and a carer who sat with the dying point the same way: it comes down to love and the people) -> the turn to hope (people found love and closeness late, when they'd stopped expecting to; it is not too late) -> a small, concrete call to act (the person you love is reachable right now; tell them) -> a closing beat that makes sending it feel natural.
- A subtle replay control and a one-tap copy/share affordance so the share moment is frictionless.

## 3. The factual bedrock (carry verbatim; this is what makes it NOT slop)
Every beat must trace to the research paper. Attribution framing matters:

**The proven pillar — the Harvard Study of Adult Development (lean here for truth).**
- The longest-running scientific study of human happiness (began 1938, 85+ years, three generations, 1,300+ descendants from 724 original participants). Its clearest, most repeated finding, in the words of its director: the people who are happiest and healthiest are those with the strongest, warmest relationships, not those with the most money or fame. Relationship quality in midlife predicts healthy aging better than wealth or class.
- The hopeful finding (for the turn): participants found love and their closest friendships late in life, in their 70s and 80s, when they had given up expecting to. "If you think it's too late for you, think again." People are not fixed by 30.
- (Internal note for honesty, not for screen: original sample was white men, broadened to descendants of both sexes over time. The finding is the strongest evidence here but this limitation is logged in the research paper.)

**The human voice — Bronnie Ware, palliative carer (use as a voice, NOT as data).**
- A carer who spent years with people in their final weeks said the same themes came up again and again: people wished they had let themselves be closer, expressed their feelings, not worked so hard at the expense of the people they loved, and lived truer to themselves. Her line, usable as the spine: it comes down to love and relationships in the end.
- Framing rule: present this as "a nurse who sat with the dying noticed..." or "those who sat with people at the end heard the same thing..." NEVER as "research shows the dying regret..." It is testimony, and it should feel like testimony, which is its own kind of power.

**The stakes — connection and health (use the direction only, no contested numbers).**
- Decades of research link strong social connection to living longer and better, and isolation to worse health. Use the *truth of the direction* (connection protects us; isolation costs us). Do NOT cite the contested "15 cigarettes" figure or any specific statistic.

**Why it must end on hope (mechanism, for the creator).**
- Research on what gets shared (Berger & Milkman, Wharton; replicated on 27,000+ articles) finds that flat sadness suppresses sharing, while content that turns toward hope, awe, or a shift in feeling is what people pass on. So the piece must move through the ache and then lift. Ending in sadness would make it true but unshared. The same research suggests restraint (an inverted-U: too much emotion backfires), so deliver the heavy truth lightly.

## 4. Success state (what "good" looks like)
- No score. Success is a stranger watching the full sequence, feeling the ache and then the lift, immediately thinking of one specific person, saving the post and sending it to them.
- The "stop scrolling" bar: the opening line must arrest a thumb mid-scroll, by being quiet, true, and personal, not by clickbait. The opposite of "you won't believe": stark and sincere.
- The share moment is the whole point. The closing beat should make sending it to someone feel like the natural, almost involuntary response.

## 5. Output (the result)
- The full cinematic sequence playing on screen.
- A replay affordance.
- A frictionless copy/share affordance.
- No account, no data capture, nothing leaves the browser.

## 6. Visual style and mood
- **Mood:** cinematic, intimate, reverent, hopeful. The closing two minutes of a film that earns its emotion honestly. A different register from LAST TEXT's late-night intimacy: bigger, slower, like a title sequence or a beautifully shot short. Earned emotion, never saccharine, never greeting-card. Restraint is the brand: the truth is heavy, so deliver it lightly.
- **Palette:** deep cinematic dark (near-black warm charcoal/midnight, #0B0C10 to #14151A) with warm soft light for text (warm off-white #F3EBDD, never harsh white). One restrained warm accent for the hopeful turn and the share affordance (soft amber/gold #E0A86B, the colour of warmth and dawn, used sparingly so it means something when it appears). The shift from cool dark toward a hint of warm light as the piece turns to hope is the visual storytelling.
- **Typography:** a beautiful emotional serif for the lines (warmth and gravity, like a film title), large, centred, generous letter- and line-spacing. A quiet sans for small UI (replay, share). The words are the entire design; the type must carry the feeling. Each line large enough to feel like a held frame.
- **Motion (the soul of it):** slow, filmic fades. Each line fades up gently, holds long enough to land (roughly 1.5-2.5s per beat, never rushed), the next arrives. A subtle drift/breath on the text so it feels alive. The transition to hope can bring a barely-perceptible warm glow. No bounce, no flashy transitions, nothing that breaks the spell. Cinematic easing only.
- **Sound:** none by default (autoplaying audio is hostile). Any optional ambient tone must be off by default and clearly toggleable. The words and motion carry the emotion, not sound.
- **Layout:** full-screen dark stage, one line at a time, centred, vast breathing room. Portrait-friendly for Instagram, comfortable on desktop. Share/replay controls quiet and out of the way until the sequence ends, then gently present.
